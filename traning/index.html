Phishing vs. Spear Phishing

Phishing (Quantity)

Broad, untargeted emails sent to thousands. Generic greetings like "Dear Customer". Low success rate per email, but high volume.

Spear Phishing (Quality)

Highly targeted. Attackers research you on social media. Uses your name, job title, and specific details. Much harder to spot.

Module 1: Expanded Attack Vectors

🚜 Pharming

"Phishing without the bait."

Redirects website traffic to a fake site using malicious code (DNS poisoning). You type bank.com, but land on a fake site without clicking a link.

🔍 Search Engine Phishing

Attackers create fake websites offering "deals" or "low interest rates" and get them indexed on Google/Bing. Users trust them because they appear in search results.

📞 Vishing & Smishing

Voice & SMS Phishing.

Example: "Netflix: We were unable to validate your billing info. Reply within 48h to avoid suspension."

🎁 Refund Scams

The allure of free money. "You have an outstanding vehicle tax refund of £92.31." These play on greed and curiosity.

🐋 Whaling

Targeting C-suite executives (CEO, CFO). These emails often threaten legal action, bankruptcy, or reputational damage to force a reaction.

👯 Clone Phishing

Resending a legitimate email you previously received, but swapping the attachment or link with malware, claiming it's an "Updated Version".

Module 2: Fake President Fraud (BEC)

A criminal poses as a high-level executive (CEO/CFO) to convince an employee to wire money. This is a 4-step psychological attack:

1

The Contact

Attacker emails from a spoofed domain (e.g., ceo@company-corp.com instead of company.com) or a "personal" email.

2

The Request

Asks for a wire transfer for a "secret acquisition", "vendor payment", or "urgent debt".

3

The Pressure

Uses Authority ("Do this now"), Secrecy ("Don't tell anyone yet"), and Time Pressure.

4

The Transfer

Employee bypasses protocol due to fear/urgency and wires funds to a foreign account.

Module 3: Spotting the Attack

The Checklist

  • ⏰ Time Sent: Was this business email sent at 3 AM?
  • 👤 Sender: Do I know them? Does the "From" address match the name?
  • 🔗 URLs: Hover over them. Do they match the company? (e.g., bestbuy1.co.uk vs bestbuy.co.uk)
  • 📝 Grammar: Legitimate companies rarely have glaring spelling errors.
  • ⚠️ Subject: Is it vague? ("Security Alert", "Invoice") or Aggressive?

Module 4: Employer Strategies

Network Segmentation

Keep sensitive information separate. If one employee falls for a phishing email, the hacker shouldn't be able to access the entire network.

Pseudonymisation

Encrypt employee and customer data. Even if a hacker steals the data, they will have a hard time making use of it.

Verify Out-of-Band

If an email asks for money, call the sender on a known number (not the one in the email) to verify the request.
